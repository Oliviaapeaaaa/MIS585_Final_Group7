UI CASE STUDY IKEA APP

The proportion of furniture purchases in e-commerce is increasing year by year, and different age groups have different purchase needs. Young people prefer a fast and convenient shopping experience to reduce unnecessary time wasted shopping.

INTRODUCTION

We redesigned the login interface and product display interface. The redesign of the IKEA app can reduce the waste of unnecessary shopping time and make the entire purchase process more streamlined. At the same time, users can still maintain their passion for shopping after browsing a large number of products.

Persona - Emily

(Our Final choice)

Emily is a 25-year-old woman, who arrived in the U.S. a few days ago. She lives alone and needs to buy furniture for her apartment. She does not have a car or valid driver's license, so she wants the furniture can be delivered.

Persona - Amy

Amy is a freshman who has just entered the university. She lives in the dormitory provided by the university. She cannot buy large furniture, only small furniture, and decorative objects. She has high requirements on the cost performance of furniture and pays attention to the appearance of furniture. The reason why she likes IKEA's offline store is that the overall experience is very good, including food and decoration style.

Persona - John

John lives in Worcester and recently moved to Boston with his family. He needs to buy furniture for his new home. His wife has high requirements for the quality and safety of the furniture, especially for the furniture in the children's room. John himself is an environmentalist and likes low-carbon life.

IKEA APP JOURNEY MAP

We created the journey map for Emily based on her scenario and her expectations. The Journey map included activities, thoughts, feels and touchpoints, It helps us to better understand milestones a user should achieve in certain stages of the process and the context from a uer’s perspective.

Emapthy map

In order to understand our user’s needs and goals in a more nuanced, we developed the Empathy Map. By looking at Emily's needs through the four lenses (says, thinks, feels, does), we can also see the complexity of their needs at times (for example, when they say one thing and do another), and find solutions that meet them where they are.

IKEA Interface Flow diagrams

We used the flowchart to model the interactions that users have with the IKEA app. Interface-flow diagrams offered a high-level view of the interface of the IKEA app, we can quickly gain an understanding of how the app is expected to work. It puts us in a position where we can validate the overall flow of your application's user interface.

Our Design - Log in

To reduce the time and steps required to register an account, we added a third-party account login function

To improve security, we have added a one-time verification code login method

Our Design - Products display

To identify specific products easily, we changed the category from a side-by-side design to a vertical list design

To constantly arouse the desire to shop, we add eye-catch points, such as comparison before and after discounts, new product arrival, inventory status (changes with info updated)

To allow users to find products quickly, we added new sorting/filtering methods such as relevance, new products, and sales

Survey and User Feedback Result

To gain a deep understanding of IKEA APP customer and their needs in purchasing furniture. We hosted interviews to gather feedback about our redesign.

60% prefer to use a third-party login, 20% prefer to log in after sign-up, a small number of people choose one-time login, and very few people think that it does not matter which login method

Most think it is more convenient, and a small number of people worry that third-party login will involve privacy issues of personal data.

70% think it is easier to find the corresponding category, and 10% of people do not often use this interface.

Most people like the highlighting of stock status, which is an important factor in weighing whether to buy this product.
